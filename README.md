# Introduction

The Glass Bead Game is a game
played with glass beads.

There are no hard-fast rules to
the Glass Bead Game.
Any who would assert
the importance of one set of rules
over another are those who
fundamentally misunderstand the game.
There are rules, though which
by cultural convention
or by explicit agreement
are common to places, times, and people.

Most forms of the game consist of
players placing beads on the table
try to create patterns,
mutually agreed to be interesting
or having some cultural significance.
The final configuration of the beads
when all moves are exhausted,
is the "score."

In the vast majority of cases,
this "score" is not competitive, but collaborative,
and does not define rankings of winners and losers,
but is best understood as a
composition -- like a musical score.
This score will frequently possess
some semantic meaning,
based on the sequence and arrangement of beads.

Consequently, the game is a meditation
on the creation of meaning
from abstract representation.

# Standard Play

The most common form of play
is based on the fictional game
described in [Hermann Hesse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Hesse)'s book,
[_The Glass Bead Game_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Glass_Bead_Game)
(sometimes called _Magister Ludi_).
While his book is intentionally vague
about the actual mechanics or meanings of the game,
his book does link the game to the
[_I Ching_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/I_Ching)
also known as the 易經 or [*Yijing]*().


Traditionally, the *Yijing* is accompanied with
different "styles" of play or performance.
The most common at the time of Zhou
involved the manipulation of yarrow stalks to
produce the final arrangement or score.
This yarrow-stalk performance is
explicitly described in Hesse's book.

For many,
Joseph Knecht's Glass Bead Game composition *Chinese House*
-- which is based on a European interpretation of this Chinese practice --
is a seminal moment in the history of the Game,
and consequently, aeons of play that follow
follow the rules and semantics of that composition.

Play is simple.
Players begin with fifty beads on a flat surface.


---

* *Yijing* - Throughout this guide, the standard pinyin term will be used to refer to the cultural phenomena of the *Yijing*,
where *I Ching* will refer to the text as translated into European languages in the 19th and 20th centuries CE,
where *Zhouyi* will refer to the most commonly translated texts, which is the oldest complete written text,
reflecting the semantic meanings used in the Western Zhou period in Chinese society (from sometime in the 10th to 4th centuries BCE).
