[thorn]
  There is, here an irony.

  A Westerner whose
  cultural heritage is credited to Greeks and Israelites,
  tries to assume the voice of
  an ancient person from the
  tradition of Zhong'gua ("the land at the center of existence").

  And surely the distance from this interpretation
  must be further off the mark than the translation
  made by a Westerner's translation of Heraclitus, or Moses, or Y'shua.

  But there is an irony also here,
  in that an interpretation or translation by any modern descendant
  of the cultural tradition of Zhong'gua
  must be done so through the lens of thousands of years of cultural lens --
  a cultural lense that is largely the shape and size dictated by Kong'zi ("Confucius")
  against who zhuang and his fellows argued ferociously against.

  It is also important to note
  that our mutual friend, siddhartha
  a descendent of the Aryan invaders of the Indus valley
  appropriated the cultural and spiritual basis of the Dravidians.

  This does not diminish his contribution.

  Nor does it diminish the later appropriation
  of the Buddhist teachings in far-off lands,
  like those in modern Java, Vietnam, China, Korea, or Japan.

  It is just a reminder that
  to be honest with oneself
  one must acknowledge these debts
  as one best can.
